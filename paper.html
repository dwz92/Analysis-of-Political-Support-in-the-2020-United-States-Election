Analysis of Political Support in the 2020 United States Election

Author

Qi Er (Emma) Teng

Published

March 19, 2024

Abstract
This paper analyzes voter behavior in Florida during the 2020 United States presidential election, employing demographic data, voter registration, and election outcomes. Through statistical analyses, it identifies key factors influencing voter support, such as ethnicity, education, and urban-rural distinctions. The study reveals demographic shifts and varying political sentiments among voter groups significantly impacted the election results, offering insights into American political dynamics and future electoral strategies.

1 Introduction

This study provides a comprehensive analysis of the political dynamics and voter behavior that characterized the 2020 United States presidential election in Florida. Utilizing a multifaceted dataset that integrates demographic information, voter registration data, and election results, this paper examines the factors that influenced Floridian support for the presidential candidates. The analysis replicates a similar regression model from previous study (Smith 2002), and highlights the significant role of ethnicity, education, and age in shaping voter preferences. Additionally, it explores the impact of socio-economic variables and local issues on the electoral outcomes. This paper contributes to the broader understanding of American political landscapes, offering insights into the evolving nature of voter behavior and the strategic considerations for future electoral contests.

2 Data and Source

This paper uses recent datasets from US Census and the R library, dataverse (U.S. Census Bureau 2023 ; Kuriwaki, Beasley, and Leeper 2023). Although this paper aims to replicate the model from the original paper (Smith 2002), we do not follow the exact model due to datasets differences and simplicity. Moreover, we used R (R Core Team 2024), a powerful statistical programming language, to clean and process our data.

3.1 Model Setup

\[\begin{equation} y_i|\pi_i \sim Bern(\pi_i) \\ \end{equation}\]

\[\begin{equation} logit(\pi_i) = \beta_0 + \beta_1(log \space population_i) + \beta_2(population \space >= \space 65_i) + \\ \end{equation}\]

\[\begin{equation} \beta_3(white_i) + \beta_4(hispanic_i) + \beta_5(black_i) + \beta_6(Biden_i) + \beta_7(Trump_i) + \end{equation}\]

\[\begin{equation} \beta_8(high_i) + \beta_9(college_i)\\ \end{equation}\]

\[\begin{align} \beta_0 \sim Norm(0, 2.5) \\ \beta_1 \sim Norm(0, 2.5) \\ \beta_2 \sim Norm(0, 2.5) \\ \beta_3 \sim Norm(0, 2.5) \\ \beta_4 \sim Norm(0, 2.5) \\ \beta_5 \sim Norm(0, 2.5) \\ \beta_6 \sim Norm(0, 2.5) \\ \beta_7 \sim Norm(0, 2.5) \\ \beta_8 \sim Norm(0, 2.5) \\ \beta_9 \sim Norm(0, 2.5) \end{align}\]

  • \(y_i\) represents the votes for Biden
  • \(log \space population_i\) represents the log population
  • \(population \space >= \space 65_i\) represents the population proportion greater or equal to 65
  • \(white_i\) represents the population proportion of white race
  • \(hispanic_i\) represents the population proportion of hispanic race
  • \(black_i\) represents the population proportion of black race
  • \(Biden_i\) represents the population proportion of voted for Biden
  • \(Trump_i\) represents the population proportion of voted for Trump
  • \(high_i\) represents the population proportion of graduated high school
  • \(college_i\) represents the population proportion of graduated college
